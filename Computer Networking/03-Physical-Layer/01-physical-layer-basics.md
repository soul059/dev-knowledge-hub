## â“ Quick Revision Questions

1. **Q:** What are the three main characteristics of a sine wave?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   The three main characteristics of a sine wave are:
   1. **Amplitude (A)** - The peak value of the signal
   2. **Frequency (f)** - Number of cycles per second (Hz)
   3. **Phase (Ï†)** - Position of the waveform relative to time 0
   </details>

2. **Q:** What is the difference between bit rate and baud rate?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   **Bit Rate** is the number of bits transmitted per second (bps). **Baud Rate** is the number of signal changes per second. The relationship is: Bit Rate = Baud Rate Ã— Bits per Signal. If each signal change represents 2 bits, then at 1000 baud, the bit rate would be 2000 bps.
   </details>

3. **Q:** What does the Nyquist theorem tell us about maximum data rate?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   The Nyquist theorem states that for a noiseless channel: **Maximum Data Rate = 2 Ã— B Ã— logâ‚‚(L)**, where B is bandwidth in Hz and L is the number of signal levels. It defines the theoretical maximum data rate for an ideal channel without noise.
   </details>

4. **Q:** What are the three types of transmission impairments?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   The three types of transmission impairments are:
   1. **Attenuation** - Loss of signal strength over distance
   2. **Distortion** - Change in signal shape due to different frequencies traveling at different speeds
   3. **Noise** - Unwanted signals added during transmission (thermal, induced, crosstalk, impulse)
   </details>

5. **Q:** What does a higher SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio) indicate?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   A higher SNR indicates that the signal power is much greater than the noise power, meaning the signal is clearer and easier to detect. This results in better quality transmission with fewer errors. For example, an SNR of 30 dB means the signal is 1000 times stronger than the noise.
   </details>

6. **Q:** What is the difference between point-to-point and multipoint line configurations?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   **Point-to-Point** has a dedicated link between exactly two devices, with full channel capacity available exclusively to them (like a cable between computer and printer). **Multipoint** has a single link shared by multiple devices, where channel capacity is divided among all connected devices (like traditional Ethernet bus topology).
   </details>
