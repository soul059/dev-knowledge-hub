1. **Q:** Why is the maximum throughput of Pure ALOHA only 18.4%?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   Pure ALOHA has a **vulnerable time of 2Ã—Tfr** (twice the frame transmission time). This is because a collision can occur if any other station starts transmitting anywhere from Tfr before to Tfr after the start of our frame. The probability of successful transmission under this condition, using Poisson distribution, gives maximum throughput S = GÃ—e^(-2G) = 0.184 (18.4%) at G = 0.5.
   </details>

2. **Q:** How does Slotted ALOHA double the throughput compared to Pure ALOHA?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   Slotted ALOHA reduces the **vulnerable time from 2Ã—Tfr to just Tfr** by requiring all transmissions to start at the beginning of a time slot. Collisions only occur when two or more stations choose the SAME slot. This halves the collision probability, leading to maximum throughput S = GÃ—e^(-G) = 0.368 (36.8%) at G = 1.
   </details>

4. **Q:** What is the hidden terminal problem and how does RTS/CTS solve it?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   **Hidden Terminal Problem**: When station A and C are both in range of B but not each other, A cannot detect C's transmission. Both may transmit to B simultaneously, causing collision at B.
   
   **RTS/CTS Solution**:
   1. Sender (A) sends RTS to access point
   2. AP broadcasts CTS (which C can hear)
   3. CTS contains duration field - C sets NAV timer
   4. C knows to stay silent during this period
   5. Collision is avoided at B
   </details>

5. **Q:** Explain Binary Exponential Backoff with an example.
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   After collision n:
   - k = min(n, 10)
   - Random number R chosen from [0, 2^k - 1]
   - Wait time = R Ã— 51.2 Î¼s (slot time)
   
   **Example**: Station experiences 3rd collision:
   - k = min(3, 10) = 3
   - R chosen randomly from [0, 7]
   - If R = 5, wait = 5 Ã— 51.2 = 256 Î¼s
   
   The exponential increase spreads out retries during high traffic, reducing repeated collisions.
   </details>

6. **Q:** Why can't wireless networks use CSMA/CD like Ethernet?
   <details>
   <summary>Answer</summary>
   Two main reasons:
   
   1. **Power Difference**: The transmitted signal is much stronger than received signals. A station cannot distinguish between its own transmission and an overlapping collision signal.
   
   2. **Hidden Terminal Problem**: A station may not be able to hear all other stations. It might sense the channel as idle while another station (hidden from it) is actually transmitting to the same receiver.
   
   Therefore, wireless uses CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance) with techniques like RTS/CTS and mandatory ACKs instead.
   </details>
