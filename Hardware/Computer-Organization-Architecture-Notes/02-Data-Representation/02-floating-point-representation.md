## â“ Quick Revision Questions

1. **Q**: Why is biased exponent used instead of 2's complement for the exponent?
   
   **A**: Biased exponent allows floating-point numbers to be compared as if they were unsigned integers (for numbers of the same sign). This simplifies comparison hardware.

2. **Q**: What is the hidden bit and why is it used?
   
   **A**: The hidden bit is the implicit leading 1 in normalized floating-point numbers (1.M). Since normalized numbers always have this 1, we don't store it, gaining one extra bit of precision.

3. **Q**: Convert 5.75 to floating-point format (sign, exponent with bias=127, mantissa).
   
   **A**: 5.75 = 101.11â‚‚ = 1.0111 Ã— 2Â². Sign=0, Exponent=2+127=129=10000001â‚‚, Mantissa=01110...

   **A**: 0.1â‚â‚€ = 0.0001100110011...â‚‚ (infinite repeating pattern). With finite mantissa bits, this must be truncated, causing representation error.

5. **Q**: If a format has an 8-bit exponent and 23-bit mantissa, how many decimal digits of precision does it provide?
   
   **A**: Precision = (23 + 1) Ã— logâ‚â‚€(2) â‰ˆ 24 Ã— 0.301 â‰ˆ 7.2 significant decimal digits.

6. **Q**: Why are floating-point numbers more densely packed near zero?
   
   **A**: The gap between adjacent representable numbers is proportional to the number's magnitude. Near zero (small exponents), numbers are closer together. As magnitude increases, the gap grows proportionally.
