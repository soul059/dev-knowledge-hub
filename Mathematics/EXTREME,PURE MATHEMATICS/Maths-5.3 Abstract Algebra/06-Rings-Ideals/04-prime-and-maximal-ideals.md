# Chapter 6.4 — Prime and Maximal Ideals

## Overview

**Prime ideals** and **maximal ideals** are the two most important classes of ideals. They connect ring theory to geometry (primes = irreducible varieties) and number theory (maximal = "points"). The key results: $R/P$ is a domain iff $P$ is prime; $R/M$ is a field iff $M$ is maximal.

---

## 1. Prime Ideals

**Definition.** An ideal $P \subsetneq R$ is **prime** if:

$$ab \in P \implies a \in P \text{ or } b \in P$$

```
  Prime ideal P:
  
  P ≠ R  and  ab ∈ P ⟹ a ∈ P or b ∈ P
  
  Analogy with prime numbers:
  p | ab  ⟹  p | a  or  p | b
  
  In ℤ:  (p) is prime  ⟺  p is prime (or p = 0)
```

**Theorem 6.11.** $P$ is a prime ideal of $R$ $\iff$ $R/P$ is an integral domain.

*Proof.* 
$(\Rightarrow)$ If $(a+P)(b+P) = ab+P = 0+P$, then $ab \in P$, so $a \in P$ or $b \in P$, meaning $a+P = 0$ or $b+P = 0$. No zero divisors.

$(\Leftarrow)$ If $ab \in P$, then $(a+P)(b+P) = 0+P$ in $R/P$. Since $R/P$ is a domain, $a+P = 0$ or $b+P = 0$, i.e., $a \in P$ or $b \in P$. $\blacksquare$

---

## 2. Maximal Ideals

**Definition.** An ideal $M \subsetneq R$ is **maximal** if there is no ideal $J$ with $M \subsetneq J \subsetneq R$.

Equivalently: $M$ is maximal among proper ideals.

**Theorem 6.12.** $M$ is a maximal ideal of $R$ $\iff$ $R/M$ is a field.

*Proof.*
$(\Rightarrow)$ Let $a + M \neq 0 + M$ in $R/M$, so $a \notin M$. Then $M + (a) = R$ (since $M + (a)$ is an ideal properly containing $M$). So $1 = m + ra$ for some $m \in M, r \in R$. Then $(r+M)(a+M) = ra + M = (1 - m) + M = 1 + M$. So $a + M$ is invertible.

$(\Leftarrow)$ If $M \subsetneq J \subseteq R$, pick $a \in J \setminus M$. Then $a + M \neq 0$ in the field $R/M$, so $\exists b$: $(a+M)(b+M) = 1+M$, giving $ab - 1 \in M \subseteq J$. Since $a \in J$ and $ab - 1 \in J$: $1 = ab - (ab-1) \in J$, so $J = R$. $\blacksquare$

---

## 3. Maximal $\implies$ Prime

**Corollary 6.13.** Every maximal ideal is prime.

*Proof.* $R/M$ is a field $\implies$ $R/M$ is a domain $\implies$ $M$ is prime. $\blacksquare$

**The converse is false** in general: $(0)$ is prime in $\mathbb{Z}$ (since $\mathbb{Z}$ is a domain) but not maximal (since $(0) \subsetneq (2) \subsetneq \mathbb{Z}$).

```
  Hierarchy:
  
  Maximal ideal  ⟹  Prime ideal  ⟹  Proper ideal
  
  R/M is a field ⟹  R/P is a domain ⟹  P ≠ R
  
  Converse fails: (0) in ℤ is prime but not maximal.
  
  Exception: In a PID, nonzero prime ⟺ maximal.
```

---

## 4. Examples in $\mathbb{Z}$

| Ideal | Prime? | Maximal? | $\mathbb{Z}/I$ |
|-------|--------|----------|----------------|
| $(0)$ | Yes ($\mathbb{Z}$ is a domain) | No | $\mathbb{Z}$ |
| $(2)$ | Yes | Yes | $\mathbb{F}_2$ |
| $(3)$ | Yes | Yes | $\mathbb{F}_3$ |
| $(4)$ | No ($2 \cdot 2 \in (4)$) | No | $\mathbb{Z}_4$ |
| $(6)$ | No ($2 \cdot 3 \in (6)$) | No | $\mathbb{Z}_6$ |
| $(p)$, $p$ prime | Yes | Yes | $\mathbb{F}_p$ |
| $(n)$, $n$ composite | No | No | $\mathbb{Z}_n$ |

**In $\mathbb{Z}$:** prime ideals = $(0)$ and $(p)$ for $p$ prime. Maximal ideals = $(p)$ for $p$ prime.

---

## 5. Examples in $F[x]$

For $F$ a field, $F[x]$ is a PID. So:

| Ideal | Prime? | Maximal? | Why |
|-------|--------|----------|-----|
| $(0)$ | Yes | No | $F[x]$ is a domain |
| $(x)$ | Yes | Yes | $x$ is irreducible |
| $(x-a)$ | Yes | Yes | $F[x]/(x-a) \cong F$ |
| $(x^2+1)$ in $\mathbb{R}[x]$ | Yes | Yes | Irreducible over $\mathbb{R}$ |
| $(x^2+1)$ in $\mathbb{C}[x]$ | No | No | $x^2+1 = (x+i)(x-i)$ |
| $(x^2-1)$ | No | No | $x^2-1 = (x-1)(x+1)$ |

**In a PID:** nonzero prime $\iff$ maximal $\iff$ generated by irreducible.

---

## 6. Examples in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$

$\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is a UFD but **not** a PID. So prime $\neq$ maximal in general.

| Ideal | Prime? | Maximal? | $\mathbb{Z}[x]/I$ |
|-------|--------|----------|-------------------|
| $(0)$ | Yes | No | $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ (domain) |
| $(2)$ | Yes | No | $\mathbb{F}_2[x]$ (domain, not field) |
| $(x)$ | Yes | No | $\mathbb{Z}$ (domain, not field) |
| $(2, x)$ | Yes | Yes | $\mathbb{F}_2$ (field!) |
| $(3, x^2+1)$ | Yes | Yes | $\mathbb{F}_3[x]/(x^2+1) \cong \mathbb{F}_9$ |
| $(x^2+1)$ | Yes | No | $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ (domain, not field) |

```
  Prime ideal lattice of ℤ[x] (partial):
  
  Maximal ideals:  (p, f(x))  where p prime, f irred. mod p
  
  Non-maximal primes:  (0), (p), (f(x))  with f irreducible
  
              (2,x)      (3,x)      (2,x+1)    (3,x²+1) ...
              / \         / \         / \         / \
           (2)  (x)    (3)  (x)    (2) (x+1)  (3) (x²+1)
              \ |       / |           \ /        / |
               (0)     (0)           (0)       (0)
```

---

## 7. Zorn's Lemma and Existence of Maximal Ideals

**Theorem 6.14.** Every ring $R \neq 0$ has at least one maximal ideal.

*Proof.* The set of proper ideals of $R$, ordered by inclusion, is non-empty (contains $\{0\}$). Every chain has an upper bound (their union). By Zorn's Lemma, a maximal element exists. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary.** Every non-unit of $R$ is contained in some maximal ideal.

---

## 8. Spectrum of a Ring

**Definition.** The **spectrum** of $R$ is:

$$\text{Spec}(R) = \{P : P \text{ is a prime ideal of } R\}$$

The **maximal spectrum** is:

$$\text{mSpec}(R) = \{M : M \text{ is a maximal ideal of } R\}$$

In algebraic geometry, $\text{Spec}(R)$ is equipped with the **Zariski topology** and studied as a geometric object.

| Ring | $\text{Spec}(R)$ |
|------|------------------|
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | $\{(0), (2), (3), (5), (7), (11), \ldots\}$ |
| $\mathbb{F}_p$ | $\{(0)\}$ |
| $\mathbb{Z}_n$ | Prime ideals = $(\bar{p})$ for primes $p \mid n$ |
| $k[x]$ ($k = \bar{k}$) | $\{(0)\} \cup \{(x-a) : a \in k\}$ |

## Summary Table

| Concept | Key Result |
|---------|-----------|
| Prime ideal $P$ | $ab \in P \implies a \in P$ or $b \in P$ |
| Maximal ideal $M$ | No ideal between $M$ and $R$ |
| $R/P$ is a domain | $\iff P$ is prime |
| $R/M$ is a field | $\iff M$ is maximal |
| Maximal $\implies$ prime | Always true |
| Prime $\implies$ maximal | True in PIDs (nonzero case), false in general |
| Existence | Every $R \neq 0$ has a maximal ideal (Zorn) |
| $\text{Spec}(R)$ | Set of all prime ideals |
