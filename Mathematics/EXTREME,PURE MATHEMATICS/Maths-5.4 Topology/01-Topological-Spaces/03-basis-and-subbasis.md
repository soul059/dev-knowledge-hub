## 1. Overview

Listing every open set in a topology is usually impractical â€” the collection is typically uncountably infinite. A **basis** is a smaller, more manageable collection from which the entire topology is generated by taking unions. A **subbasis** is even smaller: it generates a topology via finite intersections and then unions. These tools are indispensable for constructing and comparing topologies.

---

## 2. Basis for a Topology

> **Def 3.1 (Basis).** A collection $\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)$ is a **basis** for a topology on $X$ if:
>
> **(B1)** For each $x \in X$, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B$.
>
> **(B2)** If $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$ where $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$, then there exists $B_3 \in \mathcal{B}$ with $x \in B_3 \subseteq B_1 \cap B_2$.

### The Topology Generated by a Basis

> **Thm 3.2.** If $\mathcal{B}$ satisfies (B1) and (B2), then the collection
>
> $$\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}} = \left\{ U \subseteq X : \forall x \in U,\; \exists B \in \mathcal{B} \text{ with } x \in B \subseteq U \right\}$$
>
> is a topology on $X$. Equivalently, $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}$ consists of all unions of members of $\mathcal{B}$:
>
> $$\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}} = \left\{ \bigcup_{\alpha} B_\alpha : B_\alpha \in \mathcal{B} \right\} \cup \{\emptyset\}$$

**âœ¦ Key Insight:** A set is open *if and only if* it is a union of basis elements.

## 3. Standard Examples of Bases

| Topology | Set $X$ | Basis $\mathcal{B}$ |
|----------|---------|---------------------|
| Standard on $\mathbb{R}$ | $\mathbb{R}$ | $\{(a,b) : a < b,\; a,b \in \mathbb{R}\}$ |
| Standard on $\mathbb{R}^2$ | $\mathbb{R}^2$ | Open disks $B_r(x)$ or open rectangles $(a,b) \times (c,d)$ |
| Discrete on $X$ | any $X$ | $\{\{x\} : x \in X\}$ (singletons) |
| Lower-limit on $\mathbb{R}$ | $\mathbb{R}$ | $\{[a,b) : a < b\}$ (Sorgenfrey line) |

### Verification: Standard basis for $\mathbb{R}$

**(B1):** For any $x \in \mathbb{R}$, $x \in (x-1, x+1)$. âœ“

**(B2):** If $x \in (a_1, b_1) \cap (a_2, b_2) = (\max(a_1,a_2),\, \min(b_1,b_2))$, this intersection is itself an open interval (a basis element). âœ“

## 4. Recognizing a Basis for a Given Topology

> **Lem 3.3 (Munkres Lemma 13.2).** Let $(X, \mathcal{T})$ be a topological space. A collection $\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{T}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}$ if and only if:
>
> For every $U \in \mathcal{T}$ and every $x \in U$, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}$ with $x \in B \subseteq U$.

This says: $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}$ iff every open set can be "filled" by basis elements around each of its points.

## 5. Subbasis

> **Def 3.4 (Subbasis).** A collection $\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)$ is a **subbasis** for a topology on $X$ if $\bigcup_{S \in \mathcal{S}} S = X$.
>
> The topology $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{S}}$ **generated** by $\mathcal{S}$ has as basis all **finite intersections** of members of $\mathcal{S}$:
>
> $$\mathcal{B}_{\mathcal{S}} = \left\{ S_1 \cap S_2 \cap \cdots \cap S_n : S_i \in \mathcal{S},\; n \geq 1 \right\}$$
>
> Then $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{S}} = \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}_{\mathcal{S}}}$ (all unions of these finite intersections).

### Example: Subbasis for the Standard Topology on $\mathbb{R}$

Take $\mathcal{S} = \{(-\infty, b) : b \in \mathbb{R}\} \cup \{(a, \infty) : a \in \mathbb{R}\}$.

Finite intersections give: $(-\infty, b) \cap (a, \infty) = (a, b)$ for $a < b$, plus the rays themselves.

This generates the standard topology, showing the rays form a subbasis.

## 7. Worked Example: Constructing Topology from Subbasis

**Problem:** Let $X = \{1, 2, 3\}$ and $\mathcal{S} = \{\{1,2\}, \{2,3\}\}$.

**Step 1 â€” Check union covers $X$:** $\{1,2\} \cup \{2,3\} = \{1,2,3\} = X$. âœ“

**Step 2 â€” Finite intersections (build $\mathcal{B}_{\mathcal{S}}$):**
- $\{1,2\}$ (single element of $\mathcal{S}$)
- $\{2,3\}$ (single element of $\mathcal{S}$)
- $\{1,2\} \cap \{2,3\} = \{2\}$

So $\mathcal{B}_{\mathcal{S}} = \{\{1,2\}, \{2,3\}, \{2\}\}$.

**Step 3 â€” All unions (build $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{S}}$):**
- $\emptyset$ (empty union)
- $\{2\}$
- $\{1,2\}$
- $\{2,3\}$
- $\{1,2\} \cup \{2,3\} = \{1,2,3\}$
- $\{2\} \cup \{1,2\} = \{1,2\}$ (already have)
- $\{2\} \cup \{2,3\} = \{2,3\}$ (already have)

$$\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{S}} = \{\emptyset,\; \{2\},\; \{1,2\},\; \{2,3\},\; \{1,2,3\}\}$$

**Verification:** Check all three topology axioms hold. âœ“

## 8. Topology Generated by an Arbitrary Collection

> **Def 3.5.** Given **any** collection $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)$, the **topology generated by $\mathcal{A}$** is the intersection of all topologies containing $\mathcal{A}$:
>
> $$\mathcal{T}(\mathcal{A}) = \bigcap \{ \mathcal{T} : \mathcal{T} \text{ is a topology on } X,\; \mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{T} \}$$

This is the **smallest** topology making all sets in $\mathcal{A}$ open. (The intersection of topologies is always a topology â€” verify!)

If $\bigcup \mathcal{A} = X$, then $\mathcal{A}$ is a subbasis and $\mathcal{T}(\mathcal{A}) = \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$.

---

## 9. Local Basis (Neighborhood Basis)

> **Def 3.6 (Local Basis).** A collection $\mathcal{B}_x$ of neighborhoods of $x$ is a **local basis** (or **neighborhood basis**) at $x$ if for every open $U$ containing $x$, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}_x$ with $x \in B \subseteq U$.

This concept is central to the **countability axioms** (Unit 7): $(X, \mathcal{T})$ is **first countable** if every point has a countable local basis.

---

## 10. Comparing Generated Topologies

> **Lem 3.7.** Let $\mathcal{B}_1, \mathcal{B}_2$ be bases generating topologies $\mathcal{T}_1, \mathcal{T}_2$ on $X$. Then $\mathcal{T}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{T}_2$ if and only if:
>
> For every $B_1 \in \mathcal{B}_1$ and every $x \in B_1$, there exists $B_2 \in \mathcal{B}_2$ with $x \in B_2 \subseteq B_1$.

**Application:** Show that the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$ equals the topology generated by open intervals with **rational** endpoints $(p, q)$ where $p, q \in \mathbb{Q}$. (Between any $x$ and the endpoints of any standard interval, rational numbers exist by density.)
