# Unit 4: Pretexting — Topic 2: Role Playing

## Overview

**Role playing** in social engineering refers to the attacker's ability to convincingly portray a character — adopting mannerisms, vocabulary, behavior, and confidence appropriate to the assumed identity. Effective role playing transforms a pretext from a simple story into a lived performance that can withstand scrutiny and questioning.

---

## 1. Role Playing Fundamentals

```
ROLE PLAYING = PRETEXT + PERFORMANCE

PRETEXT (The Story):
  → Who you are
  → Why you're here
  → What you need

PERFORMANCE (The Execution):
  → How you speak
  → How you carry yourself
  → How you react to questions
  → How you handle unexpected situations
  → Your emotional state and confidence level

KEY PRINCIPLE:
  → The best social engineers don't "act" a role
  → They BECOME the role
  → Confidence comes from preparation
  → Authenticity comes from belief in the pretext
```

---

## 2. Character Development

```
BUILDING YOUR CHARACTER:

APPEARANCE:
  → Dress appropriately for the role
  → IT tech: polo shirt, khakis, laptop bag
  → Executive: suit, briefcase, confident posture
  → Maintenance: work clothes, tool belt, clipboard
  → Vendor: company polo, lanyard, branded materials

LANGUAGE:
  → Use appropriate jargon for the role
  → IT: "I need to push a patch," "check the AD"
  → Finance: "reconcile the ledger," "P&L review"
  → Legal: "pending litigation," "discovery process"
  → Match formality level to role and organization

BEHAVIOR:
  → Move with purpose and confidence
  → Look like you belong
  → Don't ask for permission — act like you have it
  → Engage naturally with people you encounter
  → Be friendly but not overly so

KNOWLEDGE:
  → Understand the role you're playing
  → Know common tasks/responsibilities
  → Be prepared for role-specific questions
  → Have answers for "who's your manager?"
  → Know the organization's terminology

PROPS:
  → Business cards with your identity
  → Clipboard with official-looking documents
  → Branded clothing or accessories
  → Tool bag or equipment appropriate to role
  → Phone displaying relevant information
```

## 3. Common Roles and How to Play Them

```
ROLE: IT SUPPORT TECHNICIAN
  Appearance: Business casual, laptop bag, badge
  Vocabulary: "Patch deployment," "ticket system,"
              "domain controller," "VLAN"
  Behavior: Slightly hurried, helpful but busy
  Common ask: "I need to check your machine"
  Props: Laptop, network cable, USB drive

ROLE: NEW EMPLOYEE
  Appearance: Slightly nervous, professional
  Vocabulary: "Where is..." "How do I..." "I was 
              told to come here..."
  Behavior: Confused but eager, asking for help
  Common ask: "Can you show me how to access...?"
  Props: New employee paperwork, orientation packet

ROLE: VENDOR/CONTRACTOR
  Appearance: Company polo, tool belt, work boots
  Vocabulary: "Work order," "scheduled maintenance,"
              "service agreement"
  Behavior: Purposeful, knows where to go
  Common ask: "I'm here for the [service] work"
  Props: Work order form, clipboard, tools

ROLE: EXECUTIVE
  Appearance: Suit, confident posture, expensive watch
  Vocabulary: "Board meeting," "quarterly review,"
              "stakeholder alignment"
  Behavior: In a hurry, expects compliance
  Common ask: "I need this handled immediately"
  Props: Business card, luxury items

ROLE: AUDITOR/INSPECTOR
  Appearance: Professional, clipboard, serious
  Vocabulary: "Compliance review," "audit findings,"
              "regulatory requirement"
  Behavior: Authoritative, methodical, note-taking
  Common ask: "I need to inspect/review..."
  Props: Clipboard, forms, official-looking ID
```

## 4. Handling Challenges

```
WHEN THINGS GO WRONG:

CHALLENGE: "I need to verify your identity"
  → "Absolutely, here's my card. You can also 
     call [phone number that you control]"
  → "Sure, I was sent by [real manager name from OSINT]"
  → Offer verification confidently

CHALLENGE: "I need to check with my manager"
  → "Of course, but this is time-sensitive"
  → "Your manager should have received the email"
  → "I understand. Can we proceed while you check?"
  → Don't block them — redirect

CHALLENGE: "You're not on my schedule"
  → "That's strange — the ticket was opened yesterday"
  → "There may have been a scheduling mixup"
  → "Can you check with [name]? They set this up"

CHALLENGE: "I've never seen you before"
  → "I'm new / from the other office"
  → "I usually work with [name] but they're out"
  → "Our company just won the contract"

CHALLENGE: Direct suspicion
  → Stay calm — panic confirms suspicion
  → Offer to verify through "official" channels
  → If confronted directly, have exit strategy
  → In pentesting: reveal engagement if necessary

GOLDEN RULE:
  → Never get defensive
  → Always remain calm and helpful
  → Offer solutions, not excuses
  → Know your abort trigger
```

## 5. Practice and Preparation

```
ROLE PLAY TRAINING:

PREPARATION STEPS:
  1. Research the role thoroughly
  2. Practice backstory until it's natural
  3. Rehearse with a partner
  4. Anticipate 10 tough questions
  5. Practice transitions and pivots
  6. Record yourself and review
  7. Do a dry run in safe environment

IMPROVISATION SKILLS:
  → "Yes, and..." — build on what target says
  → Listen more than talk
  → Mirror the target's communication style
  → Use silence strategically
  → Redirect questions you can't answer
  → Stay in character regardless

CONFIDENCE BUILDERS:
  → Know your material cold
  → Visualize successful outcomes
  → Start with lower-risk scenarios
  → Build from small wins
  → Debrief after every attempt
```
