# Unit 2: Reconnaissance for Social Engineering — Topic 5: Pretexting Research

## Overview

**Pretexting research** is the intelligence-gathering phase that supports building a convincing cover story (pretext) for a social engineering engagement. The research determines what role to impersonate, what knowledge to demonstrate, what language to use, and what details will make the pretext believable and difficult to challenge.

---

## 1. Research Areas for Pretexting

```
PRETEXT RESEARCH FRAMEWORK:

ROLE RESEARCH:
  → What roles commonly contact the target?
  → What authority does this role have?
  → What terminology/jargon does this role use?
  → What tools/systems does this role reference?
  → What problems would this role help solve?

  COMMON IMPERSONATION ROLES:
  Internal:
    → IT help desk / support
    → HR representative
    → Executive assistant
    → New employee
    → Auditor / compliance
    → Building maintenance
  
  External:
    → Vendor / supplier support
    → Government regulator
    → Delivery personnel
    → Phone/internet technician
    → Insurance representative
    → Consultant / contractor

LANGUAGE RESEARCH:
  → Industry-specific terminology
  → Company internal jargon
  → Reference to specific tools by name
  → Internal project names
  → Correct department names
  → Manager/colleague names to drop

TIMING RESEARCH:
  → When is the target busiest?
  → When are they most distracted?
  → When is IT maintenance typically done?
  → What are the company's business hours?
  → Are there upcoming events or deadlines?
  → When do quarterly reports/audits happen?
```

## 2. Pretext Development Process

```
BUILDING A CONVINCING PRETEXT:

STEP 1: DEFINE THE OBJECTIVE
  → What do we need? (credentials, access, info)
  → Who can provide it?
  → What would make them provide it willingly?

STEP 2: CHOOSE THE ROLE
  → Who would legitimately ask for this?
  → Who would the target not question?
  → Who has authority over this request?
  → What role is most believable?

STEP 3: BUILD THE BACKSTORY
  → Name, position, department
  → Why you're contacting them
  → Urgency or importance of request
  → Knowledge of internal details
  → How they can "verify" you (controlled channel)

STEP 4: PREPARE SUPPORTING MATERIALS
  → Fake email address (lookalike domain)
  → Phone number with correct area code
  → Business card (if in-person)
  → Badge or ID (if physical access)
  → Fake website or portal
  → Reference documents or screenshots

STEP 5: REHEARSE
  → Practice the script
  → Prepare for common objections
  → Know how to handle "I need to verify this"
  → Prepare exit strategies if challenged
  → Test technical infrastructure (email, phone)
```

---

## 3. Pretext Validation

```
TESTING YOUR PRETEXT:

BELIEVABILITY CHECKLIST:
  □ Would this person really contact the target?
  □ Does the request make logical sense?
  □ Is the urgency level realistic?
  □ Can I answer follow-up questions?
  □ Do I know enough internal details?
  □ Is my communication style appropriate?
  □ Have I prepared for the "let me verify" response?
  □ Does my infrastructure support the pretext?

COMMON PRETEXT WEAKNESSES:
  → Too much urgency (seems suspicious)
  → Not enough knowledge of company
  → Wrong communication channel for the role
  → Jargon/terminology incorrect
  → Cannot answer basic follow-up questions
  → Supporting infrastructure (email/phone) not convincing
  → Timing is wrong (calling IT at 3 AM)

RED FLAGS THE TARGET MIGHT NOTICE:
  → Email from unknown/external address
  → Phone number doesn't match company directory
  → Caller doesn't know basic company info
  → Request is unusual for the stated role
  → Excessive urgency without explanation
  → Caller gets defensive when questioned
```

## 4. Research Sources Summary

```
WHERE TO RESEARCH EACH PRETEXT ELEMENT:

COMPANY KNOWLEDGE:
  → Website about page, team bios
  → LinkedIn company page
  → Glassdoor reviews (internal culture)
  → Press releases (recent events)
  → Annual reports (strategy, departments)

ROLE KNOWLEDGE:
  → Job descriptions for that role
  → Industry forums and communities
  → Professional association websites
  → YouTube videos of the role in action
  → LinkedIn profiles of people in that role

TECHNICAL KNOWLEDGE:
  → Vendor documentation (the tool you're "supporting")
  → Online tutorials and help pages
  → Common error messages and issues
  → Support ticket templates
  → Product update announcements

LANGUAGE AND CULTURE:
  → Company social media posts (tone)
  → Employee reviews (communication style)
  → Email signatures found online
  → Internal documents (if accessible)
  → Industry conference presentations
```
